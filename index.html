EMI (Equated Monthly Instalment) is a fixed payment you make to a lender on a specific date every month. It comprises both principal repayment and interest. In the early months, a larger portion goes toward interest; over time, more goes toward the principal â€” this is called an amortising loan.

What is SIP (Systematic Investment Plan)?
A SIP is an investment strategy where you invest a fixed amount every month in a mutual fund scheme. It benefits from rupee-cost averaging (you buy more units when prices are low, fewer when high) and the power of compounding over time.

SIPs in mutual funds are market-linked and not guaranteed. However, long-term SIPs in diversified equity funds have historically delivered 10â€“14% CAGR over periods of 10+ years. Debt fund SIPs carry lower risk. Always invest based on your risk appetite and financial goals.

What is Fixed Deposit (FD)?
A Fixed Deposit (FD) is a financial instrument offered by banks/NBFCs where you deposit a lump sum for a predetermined period at a fixed interest rate. It's one of India's most popular savings instruments due to capital protection and guaranteed returns.

Yes. FD interest is added to your total income and taxed at your applicable slab rate. Banks deduct TDS at 10% if interest in a financial year exceeds â‚¹40,000 (â‚¹50,000 for senior citizens). If your income is below the taxable limit, submit Form 15G (under 60) or Form 15H (60+) to avoid TDS.

In cumulative FD, interest is compounded and paid at maturity along with principal â€” ideal for wealth creation. In non-cumulative FD, interest is paid at regular intervals (monthly, quarterly, annually) â€” ideal for those needing regular income like retirees.

Yes, most FDs can be broken prematurely with a penalty, typically 0.5%â€“1% on the applicable interest rate. Some tax-saving FDs (5-year lock-in under Section 80C) cannot be broken early. Senior citizen FDs often have waived premature withdrawal charges.

What is GST and when was it introduced?
GST (Goods and Services Tax) is a unified indirect tax that replaced multiple taxes (VAT, service tax, excise duty) across India. It was introduced on July 1, 2017. It follows a dual structure: CGST (Central) + SGST (State) for intra-state, and IGST for inter-state transactions.
What are the four GST rate slabs in India?
India has four primary slabs: 5% (essential items, unprocessed food, economy hotels), 12% (processed food, medicines), 18% (most services, electronics, FMCG), and 28% (luxury goods, tobacco, aerated drinks). A 0% rate exists for essential commodities like food grains.
What is the difference between CGST, SGST, and IGST?
CGST goes to the Central Government and SGST goes to the State Government â€” levied equally (50-50) on intra-state transactions. IGST is levied on inter-state supplies, collected by the Centre. For example, 18% GST on intra-state = 9% CGST + 9% SGST.

GST registration is mandatory if: (1) Annual turnover exceeds â‚¹40 lakh (goods) or â‚¹20 lakh (services); (2) You supply goods/services across state borders; (3) You're an e-commerce seller; (4) You were registered under old indirect tax laws. Threshold is â‚¹10L for special category states.

What does CAGR mean?
CAGR (Compound Annual Growth Rate) is the rate at which an investment would have grown if it grew at a steady rate annually. It smooths out volatility to give a single representative growth rate. It's the most widely used metric to compare investment performance across different asset classes.

How is CAGR different from IRR?
CAGR assumes a single lump sum investment, while IRR (Internal Rate of Return) handles irregular cash flows (like SIPs with varying amounts). For lump sum investments, both give similar results. For SIPs, use XIRR (a variant of IRR available in Excel/spreadsheets).

Yes. If your investment loses value over time, the CAGR will be negative. Example: â‚¹1L invested drops to â‚¹70,000 in 5 years â†’ CAGR = (0.7)^(1/5) âˆ’ 1 = âˆ’6.84%. Negative CAGR over extended periods often signals a fundamentally troubled investment.
